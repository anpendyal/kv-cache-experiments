COMPARISON WITH RUN 1 (no max_new_tokens limit, i.e. default ~50-100 tokens)

  Truncation was the dominant failure mode in Run 1; with more tokens allowed,

KEY FINDINGS
-------------
1. KVzipPress outperforms ObservedAttentionPress overall (4.7 vs 4.1), with
   the gap widening compared to Run 1 (was 3.6 vs 3.2).

2. ObservedAttentionPress has a recurring artifact: a "-1|jurisdiction:" prefix
   appears on some answers, indicating occasional instability or hallucination
   in the compressed attention representation.

3. ObservedAttentionPress hallucinates case citations. It fabricated "Hoff v.
   Zimmerman" and "Symmes v. Davis" when the correct citations were "Hoffman v.
   Savage" and "Symmes v. Drew". KVzipPress cited correctly.

4. ObservedAttentionPress produced a complete failure ("-1") on the Grand Jury
   outcome question in the 1768 case, the longest and most complex document.
   This suggests OBS struggles more than ZIP with very long contexts.

5. Both presses mischaracterized the concurring judge's suggestion in Don v.
   Soo Hoo, saying Soo Hoo should have paid back the $1,000 fee, when the
   ground truth says he should have paid Don the money she requested directly
   (a broader amount). This is a consistent factual error across both methods.

6. The 1768 Grand Jury case (super-ct-jud_1_html_0258-01) was the hardest case
   for both presses, particularly OBS (3.6 average). This is the longest
   document and contains the most complex historical and legal reasoning.


RECOMMENDED NEXT STEPS
------------------------
- Investigate why OBS produces "-1" outputs and the "-1|jurisdiction:" prefix.
- Test higher compression ratios (e.g. 0.3, 0.7) to measure sensitivity.
- Consider testing on a supported model architecture (e.g. Qwen2 or LLaMA)
  since Granite is flagged as untested by kvpress, which may contribute to
  OBS instability.
- Run a larger sample of cases to get more statistically robust results.
